Most people who walk away from a car accident feeling "okay" aren't okay. The adrenaline of the moment masks pain — sometimes for hours, sometimes for days. Whiplash, soft tissue injuries, and spinal misalignments routinely don't produce noticeable symptoms until 24 to 72 hours after the accident. By then, many people have already told the insurance company they're fine.

Getting evaluated by a chiropractor or accident physician immediately after a crash — even when you feel okay — is one of the most important things you can do for both your health and your claim.

Why Timing Matters More Than You Think

There are two reasons to seek care immediately. The first is your health: untreated whiplash and spinal injuries worsen over time. The second is your claim: gaps in treatment are used by insurance companies to argue your injuries weren't serious. An adjuster's first question when reviewing a medical file is: "Why did they wait four days to see a doctor if they were really injured?"

⚠ The Gap Problem

Any gap between your accident and your first medical visit — and any gap between appointments — weakens your case. Insurance companies track these gaps and use them to minimize or deny claims. Same-day or next-day evaluation is strongly recommended for any accident beyond a very minor fender bender.

What a Post-Accident Chiropractic Exam Actually Does

A chiropractor who specializes in accident injuries evaluates far more than your back. A thorough initial exam includes:

Cervical and lumbar spine assessment — identifying misalignments, nerve compression, and disc issues that may not yet be causing symptoms · Soft tissue evaluation — documenting muscle spasms, ligament damage, and inflammation · Range of motion testing — establishing a documented baseline for future comparison · Neurological screening — identifying any numbness, tingling, or radiculopathy · Referral coordination — connecting you with MRI, orthopedics, or specialists if needed

The documentation produced at this initial exam becomes foundational evidence in your claim. It ties your injuries directly to the accident — which is exactly why it needs to happen immediately, before the insurance company can argue the injuries came from somewhere else.

The Lien-Based Treatment Option

One of the biggest barriers people face is cost. "I don't have health insurance" or "My deductible is too high" shouldn't stop you from getting the care you need — and with accident injuries, it doesn't have to.

✓ What is Lien-Based Treatment?

Many accident-specialized physicians and chiropractors in Las Vegas work on a medical lien — meaning they treat you now and get paid from your settlement later. You pay nothing upfront. This arrangement is standard in the Nevada PI world and allows you to get the care you need regardless of your current insurance situation.

How to Choose the Right Provider

Not all chiropractors are experienced with accident cases. What you want:

Accident specialization. A provider who regularly treats accident patients understands the documentation standards required for insurance claims and legal proceedings. This is different from a general wellness practice.

Proximity to you. You'll need to attend appointments consistently — sometimes multiple times per week. A provider far from your home or work creates gaps. This is exactly what happened in my own accident, and it's why proximity is a core factor in every referral we make.

Coordination with your attorney. Your chiropractor and attorney need to communicate. Medical records, billing, lien agreements — all of this needs to flow correctly. Providers experienced with PI cases understand this process.

Common Post-Accident Injuries Chiropractic Addresses

Whiplash — the most common accident injury, caused by rapid acceleration-deceleration of the neck. Symptoms often appear 24-72 hours later. Untreated, it can become chronic.

Herniated discs — the force of a collision can push disc material out of alignment, causing pain, numbness, and weakness. Often requires MRI to diagnose properly.

Facet joint injuries — small joints in the spine that stabilize movement. Commonly injured in rear-end collisions and frequently underdiagnosed without specialist evaluation.

Muscle and ligament injuries — strains and sprains that don't appear on imaging but cause significant pain and dysfunction. Proper documentation is critical because these are easy for insurance companies to dismiss.
