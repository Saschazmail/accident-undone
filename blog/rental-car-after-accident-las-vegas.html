Who Pays for Your Rental Car

Other Driver's Fault
Their Insurance Pays
The at-fault driver's liability insurance is responsible for your rental car from the date of the accident until your vehicle is repaired or you receive a total loss settlement. The key: you must demand this coverage. They won't always volunteer it.
Your Fault or Disputed
Your Rental Coverage
If you carry rental reimbursement coverage on your own policy, it activates. Standard coverage is typically $30-50/day with a cap of 30 days. Check your policy — many people carry this without realizing it.
Uninsured Driver
Your UM Coverage
If the at-fault driver has no insurance, your own Uninsured Motorist coverage may include rental reimbursement. This varies by policy — check with your agent immediately.
No Coverage Available

One of Accident Undone's core services is arranging transportation regardless of coverage status. Rideshare coordination, rental assistance, and alternative transport — we find a solution while the coverage question gets sorted out.

How Long Are You Entitled to a Rental Car

If the other driver is at fault, you're entitled to a rental car for a reasonable period — which Nevada defines as:

For repairable vehicles: from the date of the accident until repairs are complete, plus a reasonable buffer for delays. If the body shop is backed up, you can continue rental coverage during that time.

For total loss vehicles: from the date of the accident until you receive your settlement check and have a reasonable opportunity to purchase a replacement vehicle. The insurance company cannot cut off your rental the day they declare a total loss.

⚠ Common Insurance Tactic: Early Rental Cutoff

Insurance companies frequently try to terminate rental coverage prematurely — the day the repair is "estimated" to be done (even if it isn't), the day they make a total loss offer (even if you haven't found a replacement), or after an arbitrary number of days. You have the right to challenge premature cutoffs. Document everything and escalate if needed.

What to Do If They're Dragging Their Feet

Document the delay. Get the adjuster's name and the date of every conversation. If the other driver's insurance is delaying approval of your rental, that delay is documented evidence of bad faith handling — which has legal consequences in Nevada.

Use your own coverage first if needed. If the other insurance is dragging its feet, activate your own rental coverage immediately. You can seek reimbursement from the at-fault insurance later. Don't go without transportation while they sort it out.

Keep all receipts. Every Uber, Lyft, rental receipt, or transportation cost during the period your vehicle is out of service is potentially reimbursable. Document it all.

Tourists and Visitors: Special Considerations

Las Vegas sees millions of tourists involved in accidents every year. If you're visiting and your rental car was damaged:

Your credit card may provide rental car coverage — check before you call anyone else. Many major credit cards include rental car collision coverage when you pay for the rental with that card.

The rental company's insurance coverage is typically overpriced and redundant if you have other coverage. Don't be pressured into purchasing it after the fact.

Your home state auto insurance may extend coverage to rental cars — call your agent directly, not the 800 number, and ask specifically about rental coverage in Nevada.

When Your Vehicle Is a Total Loss

The insurance company's total loss offer is almost always too low. They use proprietary valuation tools that consistently undervalue vehicles. You have the right to challenge their valuation with independent comparables — local dealer quotes, Kelley Blue Book, Edmunds, and sales data for comparable vehicles in the Las Vegas market.

During this negotiation — which can take days or weeks — your rental car entitlement continues. Do not let them pressure you into accepting a settlement by threatening to cut off your rental.
