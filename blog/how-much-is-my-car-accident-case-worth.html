How Much Is My
Car Accident Case Worth?

Las Vegas, Nevada · Updated March 2026 · 9 min read

This is the question everyone has after an accident — and almost everyone gets the wrong answer first. Usually from an insurance adjuster who has every financial incentive to tell you as little as possible.

The honest answer is: it depends on factors most people don't know to consider. Here's what actually determines case value in Nevada — and why most initial settlement offers fall far short of what the case is actually worth.

The Two Categories of Damages

Every car accident claim in Nevada involves two types of damages: economic and non-economic. Insurance companies love to settle on economic damages alone and pretend non-economic damages don't exist. They almost always do.

Economic Damages
The Bills You Can Count
Medical bills (current and future) · Lost wages · Lost earning capacity · Vehicle repair or replacement · Out-of-pocket expenses including rental car, travel to appointments, medication
Non-Economic Damages
The Costs You Can't Invoice
Pain and suffering · Emotional distress · Loss of enjoyment of life · Loss of consortium (impact on relationships) · Permanent disability or disfigurement

Non-economic damages are where most of the value lives in serious accident cases — and they're also the most commonly undervalued or ignored by insurance adjusters. There is no formula, which is exactly why insurance companies prefer to make them disappear.

The 7 Factors That Determine Your Settlement

1. Severity and Permanence of Injuries
Soft tissue injuries, whiplash, and bruising settle for far less than fractures, spinal injuries, or traumatic brain injuries. Injuries that affect you permanently — chronic pain, reduced mobility, cognitive changes — carry significantly higher values because they affect your entire future.
2. Medical Treatment Documentation
A case is only as strong as its documentation. Gaps in treatment — days or weeks where you didn't see a doctor — are used by insurance companies to argue your injuries weren't serious. Consistent, documented treatment from day one is critical.
3. Liability Clarity
How clearly is the other driver at fault? A rear-end collision at a stop light is clear liability. An intersection accident where both parties claim the other ran the light is contested. Nevada's comparative negligence rule reduces your recovery by your percentage of fault.
4. Insurance Policy Limits
Nevada's minimum liability limits are $25,000 per person. If the at-fault driver carries only minimum coverage and your damages exceed that, collecting the full amount becomes a legal challenge. Your own underinsured motorist coverage becomes critical here.
5. Impact on Daily Life and Work
Lost wages are straightforward. Lost earning capacity — if your injuries prevent you from returning to your previous occupation — is more complex and often requires expert testimony. Both are recoverable.
6. Attorney Involvement
Studies consistently show that claimants represented by attorneys recover dramatically more than those who negotiate alone — even after attorney fees. The Insurance Research Council found attorney-represented claimants received settlements 3.5 times higher on average.
7. Willingness to Go to Trial
Insurance companies track which attorneys actually take cases to trial. An attorney known for trial willingness gets better settlement offers because the insurer knows they can't just lowball and walk away. This is why our attorney matching focuses on trial-ready firms.

The first offer is always too low. It is not a starting point presented in good faith. It is a number designed to close your case before you understand what it's actually worth. No reputable PI attorney in Las Vegas would advise you to accept a first offer without thorough review.

What a Good Attorney Will Tell You

A trustworthy personal injury attorney will give you a realistic range based on your specific facts — not an inflated promise designed to win your business. If an attorney gives you a number that sounds unrealistically high in the first consultation, treat that as a red flag.

The Nevada Statute of Limitations

You have 2 years from the date of the accident to file a personal injury lawsuit in Nevada. For property damage claims, it's 3 years. These deadlines are hard — missing them means losing your right to compensation entirely, regardless of how strong your case is.

Evidence also degrades over time. Surveillance footage gets overwritten. Witnesses move. Physical evidence disappears. The strongest cases are built from the moment of the accident forward, not reconstructed months later.
