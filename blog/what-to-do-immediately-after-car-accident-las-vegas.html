What To Do Immediately
After a Car Accident

Las Vegas, Nevada · Updated March 2026 · 8 min read

The first 60 minutes after a car accident are the most critical — and most people get them wrong. Not because they're careless, but because nobody ever told them what to do. Your body is in shock. The other driver is stressed. Someone is asking if you're okay. And you're trying to remember every piece of advice you've ever half-heard about accidents.

This is the guide you needed at the scene. Read it now, before you need it.

Step 1: Stop and Stay Safe

IMMEDIATE
Move to Safety — Don't Leave the Scene

Pull over if you can do so safely. Turn on hazard lights. If the accident is minor and vehicles can be moved, get them out of traffic. Never leave the scene of an accident — in Nevada, leaving is a criminal offense. Call 911 if anyone is injured, if there's significant damage, or if the other driver seems impaired.

Step 2: Check for Injuries

Before you do anything else, check yourself and your passengers. Adrenaline masks pain. You may feel fine at the scene and wake up unable to move your neck the next morning. This happens constantly — and it's exactly why insurance companies try to get your statement and a settlement within the first 48 hours, before you understand the full extent of your injuries.

If anyone shows signs of injury, do not move them unless there's immediate danger. Call 911 immediately and let trained responders handle it.

Step 3: Call the Police

In Nevada, you are required to report any accident involving injury, death, or property damage exceeding $750. In Las Vegas, this means almost every accident. Get a police report number. This document is critical for your insurance claim and any potential legal action. Without it, the other driver's story becomes much harder to counter.

⚠ What Not to Say at the Scene

Do not apologize, even casually. Do not say "I didn't see you" or "I wasn't paying attention." Do not admit fault in any way. These statements — even innocent ones made in the shock of the moment — can be used against you. Stick to factual exchanges: name, insurance, license plate. Everything else goes through your guide or attorney.

Step 4: Document Everything

Your phone is your most powerful tool at the accident scene. Use it.

DOCUMENT NOW
What to Photograph

All vehicles from multiple angles · License plates · The full accident scene including skid marks, traffic signals, road conditions · Your injuries, even if they look minor · The other driver's license, registration, and insurance card · Any witnesses and their contact information · Weather conditions and time of day

Step 5: Exchange Information — But Say Little

Get the other driver's: full name, phone number, address, driver's license number, license plate, insurance company and policy number, vehicle make and model. Give the same in return. This is all you're legally required to share.

Do not discuss the accident, do not speculate about fault, and do not accept any offer of cash settlement at the scene. Ever. Anyone offering cash at the scene is trying to avoid their insurance company for a reason.

Step 6: Notify Your Insurance — Carefully

You're required to notify your own insurance company promptly. But be careful. Stick to the facts of what happened — date, time, location, vehicles involved. Do not give a recorded statement yet. Do not describe your injuries in detail. Do not speculate about fault. Tell them you'll follow up once you've had a chance to assess the situation.

⚠ The 30-Day Trap

Insurance adjusters are trained to make their lowest offer within the first 30 days — before you understand the full extent of your injuries or your rights. A quick settlement is almost never a fair one. Do not sign anything until you understand exactly what you're giving up.

Step 7: Seek Medical Attention — Even If You Feel Fine

This is the step most people skip — and it's the one that costs them the most. Soft tissue injuries, whiplash, and spinal injuries often don't produce symptoms for 24 to 72 hours. By the time you feel it, you may have already said you were "fine" at the scene.

The Nevada-Specific Rules You Need to Know

Statute of limitations: In Nevada, you have 2 years from the date of the accident to file a personal injury lawsuit, and 3 years for property damage claims. This sounds like plenty of time — but evidence disappears, witnesses forget, and medical documentation becomes harder to obtain the longer you wait.

Comparative negligence: Nevada uses a modified comparative negligence rule. If you're found to be 51% or more at fault, you cannot recover damages. Even partial fault reduces your recovery. This is why how you handle the scene and the initial statements matters so much.

Minimum insurance requirements: Nevada requires $25,000 per person / $50,000 per accident for bodily injury, and $20,000 for property damage. Many accidents involve damages far exceeding these minimums — which is why knowing your options matters.
