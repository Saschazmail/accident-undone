Car Accident on the
Las Vegas Strip

Las Vegas, Nevada · Updated March 2026 · 8 min read

Las Vegas is one of the most accident-prone tourist corridors in America. The Strip alone — 4.2 miles of Las Vegas Boulevard — sees thousands of accidents per year involving out-of-state drivers, rental cars, rideshare vehicles, and pedestrians. If you're visiting and you've been in an accident, the situation involves complications that residents don't face.

This guide covers exactly what to do — specifically as a tourist or visitor — from the scene forward.

The Immediate Steps — Same as Any Accident

The first steps don't change because you're a tourist: stop, check for injuries, call 911, document everything, exchange information, do not admit fault. The full immediate checklist is in our What To Do After an Accident guide.

What changes is everything that comes after.

Rental Car Complications

If you were driving a rental car, your first calls after 911 should be:

CALL 01
The Rental Company

Most rental agreements require you to notify the company of any accident, even minor ones. Failing to do so can void your coverage. Get your rental agreement number and report the incident — but do not admit fault and do not agree to any damage assessment on the spot.

CALL 02
Your Credit Card Company

Many credit cards provide primary or secondary rental car collision coverage when you pay for the rental with that card. Call the benefits line on the back of your card immediately — they often need to open a claim promptly. This coverage may pay before your personal auto insurance.

CALL 03
Your Home State Auto Insurance

Your policy from your home state typically extends coverage to rental cars nationwide. Call your agent directly to notify them and understand exactly what's covered in Nevada.

⚠ Don't Let the Rental Company Pressure You

Rental companies have a financial interest in settling damage claims quickly and on their terms. Do not sign any damage acknowledgment or loss of use agreement at the scene. Do not agree to their damage assessment without independent review. These agreements can waive your rights against the at-fault driver.

Nevada Law Applies — Regardless of Where You're From

Your home state's accident laws don't matter here. Nevada's laws govern the accident, the claim, and any legal proceedings. Key Nevada rules for tourists:

Statute of Limitations
2 Years
You have 2 years from the accident date to file a personal injury lawsuit in Nevada — regardless of where you live. This deadline does not extend because you're from out of state.
Comparative Negligence
Modified Rule
Nevada's 51% rule: if you're more than 50% at fault, you can't recover anything. Your percentage of fault reduces your recovery proportionally below that threshold.
Minimum Coverage
$25K Per Person
Nevada requires minimum liability coverage of $25,000 per person / $50,000 per accident. Many Strip accidents involve damages that exceed these minimums significantly.
Filing Location
Clark County
Any lawsuit must be filed in Nevada courts — specifically Clark County for Las Vegas Strip accidents. You can work with a Nevada PI attorney remotely from your home state.

Can You Work With a Nevada Attorney From Out of State?

Yes — and you should. A Nevada-licensed personal injury attorney handles your case regardless of where you live. You don't need to return to Las Vegas for routine case management. Depositions, medical records, and most communication happen remotely or through local representatives.

The attorneys in our network handle out-of-state clients regularly. Las Vegas's tourist-heavy accident environment means this is standard practice, not an exception.

What About Injuries You Discover After You Return Home?

This happens constantly. You leave Las Vegas feeling sore but "okay," fly home, and three days later you can't turn your head. Your medical treatment happens in your home state — but your claim is still in Nevada. This is why getting evaluated by a Las Vegas-area physician immediately after the accident is important: it establishes the connection between the accident and your injuries while you're still here. Your home-state treating physician's records become part of your Nevada claim.
